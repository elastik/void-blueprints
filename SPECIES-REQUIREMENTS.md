# Species Cultivation Requirements & Environment Specifications

> Research compiled for The Void Grows dome design (8" diameter x 10" tall chamber).
> All parameters sourced from university extension programs, mycology research, and established cultivators.
> Uncertainties are flagged explicitly. Do NOT use uncited numbers as authoritative.

## 1. Lion's Mane (Hericium erinaceus) -- FLAGSHIP SPECIES

The flagship species included with every dome. Must work perfectly in the 8" x 10" chamber.

### Temperature

| Phase | Range | Optimum | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Colonization | 68-77degF (20-25degC) | 75degF (24degC) | Mycelial growth optimum at 25degC per PMC research (PMC3755240) |
| Fruiting | 60-75degF (16-24degC) | 65-70degF (18-21degC) | Cooler temps produce denser, higher-quality fruiting bodies |

**Sources:** [The Spore Depot](https://thesporedepot.com/growing-hericium-erinaceus/), [Groovy Pigeon Mycology](https://groovypigeonmycology.com/blogs/growing-conditions/lion-s-mane-mushroom-environmental-and-growing-requirements-for-success), [PMC3755240](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3755240/)

### Humidity

| Phase | Range | Critical Minimum | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Colonization | 80-90% RH | 75% | Lower humidity acceptable during spawn run if substrate is sealed |
| Fruiting | 85-95% RH | 80% | Below 80% causes browning, stunted spines, and abort |

**Sources:** [BootstrapBee](https://bootstrapbee.com/mushrooms/lions-mane-fruiting-conditions), [Fungi Academy](https://fungiacademy.com/how-to-grow-lions-mane-mushrooms/)

### Fresh Air Exchange (FAE) & CO2

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| CO2 tolerance (fruiting) | 500-800 ppm | Below 800 ppm critical for proper pom-pom morphology |
| CO2 tolerance (colonization) | Up to 5,000+ ppm | High CO2 acceptable during spawn run |
| Air exchange frequency | 5-8 exchanges/hour | High FAE required -- lion's mane is CO2-sensitive |
| CO2 stress symptoms | Short spines, elongated stalk, coral-like branching instead of single pom-pom | Extremely diagnostic -- malformed fruit = too much CO2 |

**CRITICAL NOTE:** Lion's mane is one of the most CO2-sensitive gourmet species. The dome's passive airflow vents must be designed to keep CO2 well below 1,000 ppm during fruiting. This is the single most important design constraint for the flagship species.

**Sources:** [BootstrapBee](https://bootstrapbee.com/mushrooms/lions-mane-fruiting-conditions), [Atlas Scientific](https://atlas-scientific.com/blog/why-you-need-a-co2-meter-for-growing-mushrooms/), [Liquid Fungi](https://liquidfungi.com/pages/special-considerations-when-growing-lions-mane-mushrooms)

### Light Requirements

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Wavelength | 430-470nm (blue spectrum) | Blue light photoreceptors trigger pinning and development |
| Intensity | 200-500 lux | Gentle indirect light preferred; 200-400 lux optimal range |
| Duration | 12h on / 12h off | Diurnal cycle triggers pinning genes |
| Color temperature | 5000-6500K | Cool white / daylight spectrum |

**Sources:** [Active Grow LED](https://activegrowled.com/pages/why-blue-light-is-best-for-growing-mushrooms), [Mushroom Enthusiast](https://www.mushroomenthusiast.com/all-about-mushroom-grow-lights/)

### Substrate Preferences

| Substrate | Suitability | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Master's Mix (50/50 hardwood sawdust + soy hulls) | **Best** | Produces largest, densest fruiting bodies |
| Hardwood sawdust + 10-20% wheat bran | Excellent | Traditional commercial substrate |
| Hardwood sawdust + 10-20% rice bran | Excellent | PMC research confirms optimal mycelial growth |
| Straw | Poor | Not recommended for lion's mane |

Hydration: 60-65% moisture content. Must be sterilized (pressure cook 15 PSI, 2+ hours).

**Sources:** [GroCycle (Master's Mix)](https://grocycle.com/what-is-masters-mix/), [Mushroom Substrate](https://www.mushroomsubstrate.com/blogs/mushroom-substrate-blog/lions-mane-mushroom-substrate-recipe), [PMC3755240](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3755240/)

### Growth Timeline

| Stage | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Colonization (spawn run) | 14-21 days | At 75degF; full white mycelium coverage |
| Primordia formation (pinning) | 3-7 days | Triggered by fresh air, light, and temperature drop |
| Fruiting body development | 5-10 days | Spines elongate; harvest when spines begin to droop |
| Total (inoculation to harvest) | 22-38 days | Fastest of the non-oyster gourmet species |
| Flushes | 2-3 | Subsequent flushes smaller; soak block between flushes |

**Sources:** [Lykyn](https://lykyn.com/blogs/news/lions-mane-mushroom-growth-stages), [The Spore Depot](https://thesporedepot.com/growing-hericium-erinaceus/)

### Dome Compatibility Notes (8" x 10" Chamber)

- **Size:** Lion's mane fruiting bodies can reach 4-8" diameter in optimal conditions. The 8" dome diameter should accommodate a single fruiting body well, but may constrain very large specimens.
- **CO2 is the critical constraint.** The small chamber volume (~400 cubic inches) will accumulate CO2 quickly from a respiring substrate block. Passive vents must be carefully sized and positioned.
- **Humidity retention:** The enclosed dome should maintain humidity well. Risk of over-humidification (condensation dripping on fruit body) rather than under-humidification.
- **Substrate block size:** A standard 5 lb fruiting block will NOT fit in the dome. Void Packs must be custom-sized -- likely 1-2 lb blocks optimized for the chamber volume.
- **Recommended as included species:** Yes -- flagship. Chamber must be optimized for this species first.

## 2. Blue Oyster (Pleurotus ostreatus var. columbinus)

The most forgiving species. Beginner-friendly and fast-growing.

### Temperature

| Phase | Range | Optimum | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Colonization | 65-75degF (18-24degC) | 75degF (24degC) | Fast colonizer at warm room temp |
| Fruiting | 45-65degF (7-18degC) | 55-60degF (13-16degC) | Cold-loving species; prefers cooler fruiting temps than other oysters |

**NOTE:** Blue oyster's preference for cool fruiting temperatures (45-65degF) is significantly lower than the other target species. This creates a design tension -- the dome may need to accommodate species with very different temperature preferences. Blue oyster will fruit at room temperature but produces smaller, thinner caps with longer stems.

**Sources:** [North Spore](https://northspore.com/blogs/the-black-trumpet/how-to-grow-blue-oyster-mushrooms), [The Spore Depot](https://thesporedepot.com/growing-blue-oyster-mushrooms/), [GroCycle](https://grocycle.com/blue-oyster-mushroom/)

### Humidity

| Phase | Range | Critical Minimum | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Colonization | 80-90% RH | 75% | Substrate in sealed bag; ambient not critical |
| Fruiting | 85-95% RH | 80% | Pins abort below 80%; caps crack and curl |

**Sources:** [The Mushroom Box](https://themushroombox.shop/blogs/the-mushroom-blog/what-you-need-to-know-about-blue-oyster-mushrooms), [GroCycle](https://grocycle.com/how-to-grow-oyster-mushrooms/)

### Fresh Air Exchange (FAE) & CO2

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| CO2 tolerance (fruiting) | < 800 ppm | Very CO2-sensitive; will produce "fuzzy feet" (elongated stems, tiny caps) |
| CO2 tolerance (colonization) | Up to 10,000+ ppm | High CO2 acceptable during spawn run |
| Air exchange frequency | 4-6 exchanges/hour | Slightly less demanding than lion's mane but still needs strong FAE |
| CO2 stress symptoms | Elongated stems, small caps ("fuzzy feet"), spongy texture | The classic sign of insufficient FAE in oyster cultivation |

**Sources:** [Zombie Myco](https://zombiemyco.com/blogs/mushroom-teks/co2-meter-for-mushrooms-do-you-really-need-one), [The Spore Depot](https://thesporedepot.com/growing-blue-oyster-mushrooms/)

### Light Requirements

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Wavelength | 430-470nm (blue spectrum) | Blue light triggers pinning |
| Intensity | 500-1,000 lux | Moderate indirect light; can tolerate up to 3,000 lux |
| Duration | 12h on / 12h off | Standard diurnal cycle |

**Sources:** [Mobile Mycology](https://www.mobilemycology.com/optimizing-light-intensity-and-spectrum-for-mushroom-cultivation/), [La Mycosphere](https://lamycosphere.com/en-int/blogs/the-future-is-fungi/do-mushrooms-need-light)

### Substrate Preferences

| Substrate | Suitability | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Straw (pasteurized) | **Best** | Fastest colonization; natural preference |
| Hardwood sawdust + supplements | Excellent | Works well with Master's Mix |
| Coffee grounds (supplemented) | Good | Budget option; higher contamination risk |
| Cardboard | Fair | Slow but works for small-scale |

Straw preparation: hot water pasteurization at 160-170degF (71-77degC) for 1-2 hours.

**Sources:** [North Spore](https://northspore.com/blogs/the-black-trumpet/how-to-grow-blue-oyster-mushrooms), [GroCycle](https://grocycle.com/how-to-grow-oyster-mushrooms/)

### Growth Timeline

| Stage | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Colonization (spawn run) | 10-14 days (sawdust), 14-21 days (straw) | Fast colonizer |
| Pinning | 3-5 days | Triggered by FAE, light, and temperature drop |
| Fruiting body development | 5-7 days | Rapid cluster formation |
| Total (inoculation to harvest) | 18-26 days | Among the fastest gourmet species |
| Flushes | 2-4 | Good repeat fruiter |

**Sources:** [EZMushroom](https://ezmushroom.com/grow/how-to-grow-blue-oyster-mushrooms/), [North Spore](https://northspore.com/blogs/the-black-trumpet/how-to-grow-blue-oyster-mushrooms)

### Dome Compatibility Notes (8" x 10" Chamber)

- **Size:** Blue oyster clusters grow laterally from the substrate. Multiple clusters may emerge from a single block. Clusters can reach 4-6" spread.
- **Growth habit:** Oysters grow FROM the substrate surface toward light and air. The dome needs adequate clearance and the substrate block should be positioned so clusters grow upward, not pressed against the dome wall.
- **Temperature concern:** Blue oyster prefers cooler temps (45-65degF for fruiting) than room temperature. At typical indoor temps (68-72degF), expect leggy stems and smaller caps. Still viable but not optimal.
- **FAE sensitivity:** Will show "fuzzy feet" quickly in the small dome volume if vents are insufficient.
- **Recommended as beginner species:** Yes -- extremely forgiving. Even sub-optimal conditions produce some harvest.

## 3. Pink Oyster (Pleurotus djamor)

Tropical species with stunning visual appeal. Fast-growing and warm-loving.

| Phase | Range | Optimum | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Colonization | 75-86degF (24-30degC) | 77-80degF (25-27degC) | Warm -- tropical origin |
| Fruiting | 68-86degF (20-30degC) | 75-80degF (24-27degC) | One of the few species that fruits well at warm room temp |

**IMPORTANT:** Pink oyster mycelium dies below 45degF (7degC). Do NOT refrigerate cultures or substrate. This is a critical handling consideration for Void Pack shipping and storage.

**Sources:** [Mushroom Farm Supplies](https://mushroomfarmsupplies.com.au/pink-oyster-mushroom-fruiting-conditions/), [Agripie](https://www.agripie.com/post/how-to-grow-pleurotus-djamor-mushrooms-effectively)

| Flushes | 2-3 | Quick successive flushes |

**Sources:** [GroCycle](https://grocycle.com/pink-oyster-mushroom/), [The Spore Depot](https://thesporedepot.com/growing-pink-oyster-mushrooms/)

### Dome Compatibility Notes (8" x 10" Chamber)

- **Visual appeal:** Vibrant coral-pink color makes this species particularly striking under UV-A lighting. Key visual species for marketing.
- **Temperature fit:** Fruits well at room temperature (68-80degF), making it ideal for an unheated desktop dome.
- **Speed:** Fastest species in the lineup -- good for first-time users who want quick results.
- **Shipping constraint:** Cannot be exposed to temperatures below 45degF. Void Pack shipping must account for cold weather.
- **Cluster size:** Pink oyster clusters are typically smaller than blue oyster, fitting well within the dome.
- **Short shelf life:** Fruits deteriorate quickly after harvest (1-2 days). Encourage users to harvest and consume immediately.

## 4. Shiitake (Lentinula edodes)

Culinary staple with more complex cultivation requirements. Rewards patience.

### Temperature

| Phase | Range | Optimum | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Colonization | 68-80degF (20-27degC) | 75degF (24degC) | Standard warm colonization |
| Browning (maturation) | 60-70degF (16-21degC) | 65degF (18degC) | Unique stage: block turns brown over 4-8 weeks |
| Fruiting initiation | Cold shock: 35-50degF (2-10degC) for 12-24h | 40degF (4degC) | Cold shock triggers pinning; refrigerate block |
| Fruiting | 55-70degF (13-21degC) | 60-65degF (16-18degC) | Cooler fruiting produces higher quality caps |

**NOTE:** Shiitake has a unique "browning" stage between colonization and fruiting. The block must turn 75%+ brown (8-12 weeks after inoculation) before fruiting is initiated. This is NOT contamination -- it is a melanized protective layer. The cold shock requirement may be difficult to achieve in a passive desktop dome without user intervention (e.g., placing block in refrigerator before returning to dome).

**Sources:** [FreshCap](https://learn.freshcap.com/growing/growing-shiitake-mushrooms/), [GroCycle](https://grocycle.com/how-to-grow-shiitake-mushrooms/), [Unicorn Bags](https://unicornbags.com/cultivation/lentinula-edodes/)

### Humidity

| Phase | Range | Critical Minimum | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Colonization | 90-100% RH | 85% | Sealed bag; high humidity for spawn run |
| Browning | 70-80% RH | 60% | Reduced humidity during maturation |
| Fruiting (early) | 85-95% RH | 80% | High humidity during pin development |
| Fruiting (late) | 60-80% RH | 55% | Reduce humidity as caps mature to prevent waterlogging |

**Sources:** [Unicorn Bags](https://unicornbags.com/cultivation/lentinula-edodes/), [Mushroom Farm Supplies](https://mushroomfarmsupplies.com.au/shiitake-3782-fruiting-conditions/)

### Fresh Air Exchange (FAE) & CO2

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| CO2 tolerance (fruiting) | < 1,000 ppm (optimal 600-800 ppm) | Moderate sensitivity |
| CO2 tolerance (colonization) | 10,000+ ppm | Minimal air exchange during spawn run |
| Air exchange frequency | 4-8 exchanges/hour during fruiting | Standard range |
| CO2 stress symptoms | Elongated stems, thin caps | Less dramatic than oyster species |

**NOTE:** Shiitake is more CO2-tolerant than oyster species during fruiting. It will produce acceptable-looking fruits at slightly elevated CO2 levels, though quality improves with better ventilation.

**Sources:** [Unicorn Bags](https://unicornbags.com/cultivation/lentinula-edodes/), [CO2 Meter](https://www.co2meter.com/blogs/news/co2-mushroom-farming)

### Light Requirements

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Wavelength | 430-470nm (blue spectrum) | Blue light important for cap formation |
| Intensity | 500-2,000 lux | Higher light requirements than oyster species |
| Duration | 12h on / 12h off | Standard diurnal cycle |

**NOTE:** Shiitake has among the highest light requirements of the target species. The dome's UV-A LEDs may not provide sufficient blue-spectrum light for optimal cap development. Consider supplemental white/blue LEDs or ensure UV-A output partially overlaps the blue range.

**Sources:** [Unicorn Bags](https://unicornbags.com/cultivation/lentinula-edodes/), [Active Grow LED](https://activegrowled.com/pages/why-blue-light-is-best-for-growing-mushrooms)

### Substrate Preferences

| Substrate | Suitability | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hardwood sawdust + 10-20% wheat bran | **Best** | Industry standard for indoor shiitake blocks |
| Hardwood sawdust + 10-20% rice bran | Excellent | Common Asian production method |
| Oak logs (outdoor) | Traditional | Not applicable for dome |
| Master's Mix | Good | Works but some growers report odd-looking fruits with high supplementation |

Add 1-3% gypsum (calcium sulfate) for pH buffering. Must be sterilized.

**Sources:** [FreshCap](https://learn.freshcap.com/growing/growing-shiitake-mushrooms/), [Mushroom Substrate](https://www.mushroomsubstrate.com/blogs/mushroom-substrate-blog/how-to-grow-shiitake-mushrooms), [PMC10456065](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10456065/)

### Growth Timeline

| Stage | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Colonization (spawn run) | 30-45 days | Slower than oyster or lion's mane |
| Browning (maturation) | 30-60 days | Block turns brown; must reach 75%+ brown |
| Cold shock | 12-24 hours | Refrigerate or cold water soak |
| Pinning | 5-7 days | After cold shock + return to fruiting conditions |
| Fruiting body development | 7-14 days | Slower fruit development than oyster |
| Total (inoculation to first harvest) | 75-130 days | Significantly longer than other target species |
| Flushes | 2-4 | Soak block 12-24h between flushes |

**Sources:** [FreshCap](https://learn.freshcap.com/growing/growing-shiitake-mushrooms/), [GroCycle](https://grocycle.com/how-to-grow-shiitake-mushrooms/)

### Dome Compatibility Notes (8" x 10" Chamber)

- **Long timeline:** 2.5-4+ months from inoculation to first harvest. This is a patience species. Marketing must set expectations clearly.
- **Cold shock required:** Users must remove the block from the dome, refrigerate it for 12-24 hours, then return it. This is an active step that breaks the "set it and forget it" simplicity of the dome.
- **Browning stage:** The block will look brown and potentially alarming to beginners for weeks. Documentation must explain this is normal and necessary.
- **Cap size:** Individual shiitake caps are 2-4" diameter, fitting well within the dome.
- **Void Pack design:** Shiitake Void Packs should be pre-colonized AND pre-browned (ready for cold shock and fruiting). This skips the 2-3 month wait and makes the dome experience much more practical. Ship the block already at the browning stage.
- **Higher light needs:** May require supplemental blue/white LED in dome design.

## 5. Reishi (Ganoderma lucidum)

Medicinal species. Slow-growing but visually dramatic. Produces either antler or conk forms depending on CO2 levels.

### Temperature

| Phase | Range | Optimum | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Colonization | 75-86degF (24-30degC) | 80degF (27degC) | Warm colonization; slower at room temp |
| Fruiting | 68-80degF (20-27degC) | 72-75degF (22-24degC) | Moderate; fruits well at room temperature |

**Sources:** [Out-Grow](https://www.out-grow.com/blogs/growing-mushrooms/how-to-grow-reishi-mushrooms), [GroCycle](https://grocycle.com/grow-reishi-mushrooms/), [FreshCap](https://learn.freshcap.com/growing/candidates-for-cultivation-the-reishi-mushroom/)

### Humidity

| Phase | Range | Critical Minimum | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Colonization | 85-95% RH | 80% | High humidity during spawn run |
| Fruiting | 80-90% RH | 75% | Conk form needs slightly less humidity than antler |

**Sources:** [Out-Grow](https://www.out-grow.com/blogs/growing-mushrooms/how-to-grow-reishi-mushrooms), [Fungi Academy](https://fungiacademy.com/how-to-grow-reishi-mushrooms/)

### Fresh Air Exchange (FAE) & CO2

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| CO2 for **antler form** | > 2,000 ppm | Low FAE produces coral/deer-antler formations |
| CO2 for **conk form** | < 1,000 ppm | High FAE produces traditional shelf/fan shape |
| Air exchange for conks | 4-6 exchanges/hour | Standard fruiting FAE |

**DESIGN NOTE:** Reishi's CO2-dependent morphology is a feature, not a bug. In the dome:
- **Default (passive vents):** Likely produces antler form due to higher CO2 in the small chamber. Antler reishi is visually stunning -- coral-like structures that look alien. This may actually be the preferred form for a desktop display piece.
- **With increased ventilation:** Conk (shelf) form. Wider, flatter, lacquered surface.
- The dome could market both forms: "Open the vents for shelves. Close them for antlers."

**Sources:** [FreshCap](https://learn.freshcap.com/growing/candidates-for-cultivation-the-reishi-mushroom/), [Lykyn](https://lykyn.com/blogs/news/antler-reishi-mushroom), [The Spore Depot](https://thesporedepot.com/growing-reishi-ganoderma-lingzhi-mushrooms/)

### Light Requirements

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Wavelength | Blue spectrum (430-470nm) | Standard fruiting trigger |
| Intensity | 750-1,500 lux | Higher light requirements; among the highest of target species |
| Duration | 12h on / 12h off | Standard diurnal cycle |

**NOTE:** Reishi has high light requirements (750-1,500 lux) compared to lion's mane (200-500 lux). The dome lighting may need adjustable intensity, or the UV-A/aesthetic lighting should be supplemented with a brighter blue/white LED mode for species that need more lux.

**Sources:** [DMR Solan (PDF)](https://dmrsolan.res.in/Folder_Ganoderma.pdf), [Mushroology](https://mushroology.com/how-to-grow-ganoderma-lucidum-reishi-mushrooms/)

### Substrate Preferences

| Substrate | Suitability | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hardwood sawdust + 20% wheat bran | **Best** | Oak, maple, walnut sawdust preferred |
| Master's Mix | Good | Works well for indoor cultivation |
| Hardwood logs (outdoor) | Traditional | Not applicable for dome |

Add gypsum or calcium carbonate for pH buffering. Must be sterilized.

**Sources:** [Redwood Mushroom Supply](https://www.redwoodmushroomsupply.com/blogs/mushroom-cultivation/how-to-grow-reishi-mushrooms), [GroCycle](https://grocycle.com/grow-reishi-mushrooms/)

### Growth Timeline

| Stage | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Colonization (spawn run) | 14-30 days | Moderate colonization speed |
| Pinning | 7-14 days | Slower to initiate than oyster |
| Fruiting body development (antler) | 30-60 days | Antlers grow slowly upward |
| Fruiting body development (conk) | 45-90 days | Conks develop slowly, build lacquered surface |
| Total (inoculation to harvest) | 60-180 days (2-6 months) | Very slow species |
| Flushes | 1-2 | Limited reflush; reishi typically exhausts substrate |

**Sources:** [FreshCap](https://learn.freshcap.com/growing/candidates-for-cultivation-the-reishi-mushroom/), [Out-Grow](https://www.out-grow.com/blogs/growing-mushrooms/how-to-grow-reishi-mushrooms)

### Dome Compatibility Notes (8" x 10" Chamber)

- **Slow but dramatic:** Reishi is a living sculpture species. Antler forms in particular look alien and would be stunning under UV-A light. Market as a "living art" species -- the slow growth IS the experience.
- **Antler form is dome-native:** The small chamber volume will naturally produce higher CO2, encouraging antler form. This is actually ideal for the dome aesthetic.
- **Vertical growth:** Antler reishi grows upward. The 10" dome height should accommodate typical antler growth, but very vigorous strains could potentially reach the dome ceiling.
- **Long commitment:** Users must understand this is a 2-6 month grow. Position as the "advanced" or "patience" Void Pack.
- **Medicinal appeal:** Primary value is medicinal (adaptogen, immune support). Dried and powdered for tea or tincture. Marketing angle: "Grow your own medicine."
- **Humidity and heat:** Reishi's tolerance for warmer temps (68-80degF fruiting) makes it well-suited to an indoor dome without cooling.

## UV-C Sterilization Research

> **CRITICAL DISTINCTION:** UV-C (germicidal, 200-280nm) and UV-A (aesthetic blacklight, 365-405nm) are completely different systems serving completely different purposes. UV-C kills microorganisms and is dangerous to humans. UV-A creates the purple glow aesthetic and is safe for viewing. They must NEVER be conflated in marketing, documentation, or user experience.

### UV-C Wavelength & Germicidal Effectiveness

| Wavelength | Type | Effectiveness | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 254nm | Mercury vapor lamp (traditional) | Gold standard; most research data available | Being phased out due to mercury content |
| 265nm | LED | Peak germicidal efficiency; 3x more effective than 270-280nm | Highest disinfection per watt |
| 275nm | LED | Excellent germicidal effectiveness | Most commercially available UV-C LED wavelength |
| 222nm | Far-UVC (excimer lamp) | Effective; potentially safer for incidental human exposure | Emerging technology; higher cost; limited LED availability |

**Recommendation for The Void dome:** 275nm UV-C LED. This wavelength offers excellent germicidal effectiveness, is the most commercially available UV-C LED wavelength, and has established supply chains for consumer product integration.

**Sources:** [Luminus UV-C Application Note (PDF)](https://download.luminus.com/datasheets/APN-002805_Rev02.2_UVC_LEDs_for_Disinfection_and_Sterilization.pdf), [Waveform Lighting](https://www.waveformlighting.com/uv-c-led/uv-c-led-lighting-for-germicidal-sterilization-and-disinfection-applications), [Super Lighting LED](https://www.superlightingled.com/265nm280nm-efficient-sterilization-smd3535-uv-c-led-30pcspack-p-5369.html)

### Dosage Requirements for Common Contaminants

UV-C dosage is expressed in mJ/cm2 (millijoules per square centimeter). Higher doses provide more thorough inactivation.

| Contaminant | Dose for 90% kill (1-log) | Dose for 99% kill (2-log) | Dose for 99.9% kill (3-log) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Trichoderma sp.** (green mold) | ~20-25 mJ/cm2 | **45 mJ/cm2** | ~65-80 mJ/cm2 (estimated) | Primary mushroom cultivation contaminant; relatively UV-resistant |
| **Aspergillus fumigatus** | 54 mJ/cm2 (90%) | ~110 mJ/cm2 | ~165 mJ/cm2 | Common indoor mold |
| **Aspergillus niger** (black mold) | ~80 mJ/cm2 | ~175 mJ/cm2 | **528 mJ/cm2** (99.9999%) | Most UV-resistant common mold; very high doses needed for full kill |
| **Aspergillus flavus** | 35 mJ/cm2 (90%) | ~70 mJ/cm2 | **240 mJ/cm2** (99.9999%) | Aflatoxin-producing mold |
| **Penicillium sp.** | ~100 mJ/cm2 | **220 mJ/cm2** | ~350 mJ/cm2 | Blue/green mold; common contaminant |
| **E. coli** (bacteria reference) | 3-5 mJ/cm2 | 6-8 mJ/cm2 | ~10 mJ/cm2 | Bacteria are much easier to kill than fungal spores |

**Key finding:** Fungal spores are significantly more UV-resistant than bacteria. The dome's UV-C system must target the hardest contaminants (Trichoderma, Aspergillus) which require 45-528 mJ/cm2 for effective inactivation. A design target of **100 mJ/cm2** would achieve 99% kill of Trichoderma and significant reduction of most Aspergillus species, though A. niger requires much higher doses for complete sterilization.

**Uncertainty note:** Exact dose-response data for some species varies between studies due to differences in spore preparation, UV-C wavelength, and measurement methodology. The values above represent consensus ranges from multiple sources. The 3-log doses for Trichoderma are estimated from the 2-log data; precise 3-log data was not found in available literature.

**Sources:** [UV Light Technology dosage table](https://uv-light.co.uk/uv-dosage-required-to-kill-microorganisms/), [Ultraviolet.com inactivation data](https://ultraviolet.com/microorganisms-inactivated/), [ScienceDirect (Aspergillus research)](https://cdnsciencepub.com/doi/10.1139/w04-002), [PMC11218994 (Far-UVC mold study)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11218994/), [ScienceDirect (Trichoderma)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1385894717312020)

### UV-C LED Specifications for 8" Dome

| Parameter | Specification | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Wavelength** | 275nm | Best commercial availability; excellent germicidal effectiveness |
| **LED count** | 1-2 LEDs | Small chamber volume (~400 cubic inches) requires minimal source |
| **Power per LED** | 1-3W electrical input, 10-40mW UV-C output | Typical efficiency of UV-C LEDs is 1-5% (wall plug efficiency) |
| **Beam angle** | 120deg | Wide angle for maximum surface coverage in dome |
| **Placement** | Top-center of dome (lid/cap) pointing downward | Illuminates substrate surface and dome interior walls |
| **Exposure time** | 15-30 minutes per cycle | At ~0.1 mW/cm2 irradiance, 15 min = ~90 mJ/cm2 dose |
| **Operating voltage** | 5-8V DC (compatible with USB-C power) | Integration with existing power system |
| **Lifetime** | ~9,000 hours typical | Adequate for periodic sterilization cycles |

**Dose calculation for the dome:**
- Dome interior surface area: approximately 550-600 cm2 (8" diameter hemisphere + base area)
- At 8" (20cm) distance, a single 40mW UV-C LED with 120deg beam produces approximately 0.05-0.15 mW/cm2 irradiance (varies by position)
- 15-minute exposure: 0.1 mW/cm2 x 900 seconds = **90 mJ/cm2**
- This achieves 99% kill of Trichoderma and significant reduction of most contaminants
- 30-minute exposure doubles the dose to ~180 mJ/cm2 for more resistant species
- **Uncertainty:** These are engineering estimates. Actual irradiance distribution depends on LED optics, dome geometry, and surface reflectivity. Prototype testing required.

**Sources:** [LED Supply (Seoul Viosys module)](https://www.ledsupply.com/leds/uv-c-275nm-seoul-viosys-led), [TME Electronics](https://www.tme.com/us/en-us/news/library-articles/page/42464/leds-for-sterilization-with-uv-c-light/), [Waveform Lighting](https://www.waveformlighting.com/uv-c-led/how-to-determine-uv-c-irradiance-requirements-for-disinfection-applications)

### Safety Requirements -- UV-C is DANGEROUS

UV-C radiation is harmful to human eyes and skin. This is not optional safety theater -- UV-C causes real injury.

#### Human Exposure Limits

| Parameter | Limit | Source |
|---|---|---|

### Impact on Mushrooms: UV-C and Mycelium

| Scenario | Effect | Safe? |
|---|---|---|
| **UV-C on active mycelium** | Damages DNA; kills surface mycelium; can cause mutations | **NO** -- never expose during active growth |
| **UV-C on fruiting bodies** | Damages tissue; alters mechanical properties of fruit | **NO** -- never expose during fruiting |

## UV-A / Blacklight Aesthetic Lighting

> This section covers the UV-A system -- the aesthetic purple glow that defines The Void Grows brand. UV-A is safe, visible, and runs continuously during the grow cycle. It is NOT germicidal and should NEVER be marketed as sterilization.

### UV-A Wavelength Specifications

| Parameter | Option A: 365nm | Option B: 395nm | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Classification** | UV-A (near-UV) | UV-A / visible violet border | -- |
| **Visible appearance** | Dull bluish-white; minimal visible glow | Strong purple/violet glow | 395nm for aesthetics |
| **Fluorescence excitation** | Stronger fluorescence in target materials | Moderate fluorescence; more visible light | 365nm for fluorescence |
| **Safety** | Safe for indirect viewing; avoid prolonged direct eye exposure | Safer than 365nm; lower UV energy | Both acceptable |
| **Cost** | Higher (less efficient LED chips) | Lower (more efficient, mainstream) | 395nm is more cost-effective |
| **LED efficiency** | Lower wall-plug efficiency | Higher efficiency | 395nm advantage |
| **Visible purple glow** | Weak | **Strong** | 395nm matches brand aesthetic |

**Recommendation: Use 395nm as the primary UV-A LED for the aesthetic glow.**

Rationale:
- The brand is built on the visible purple glow aesthetic. 395nm produces a strong, visible violet/purple light that photographs well and creates the "alien" atmosphere.
- 365nm would produce stronger fluorescence effects but weaker visible glow, and costs more.
- **Hybrid option:** Use 395nm for the primary aesthetic glow + 1-2 supplemental 365nm LEDs for enhanced fluorescence effects. This provides both the visible purple atmosphere AND the fluorescence excitation. The Void Core (smart version) could allow users to toggle between modes.

**Sources:** [Waveform Lighting (365 vs 395nm)](https://www.waveformlighting.com/tech/what-is-the-difference-between-365-nm-and-395-nm-uv-led-lights), [Super Lighting LED](https://www.superlightingled.com/blog/uva-black-light-365nm-vs-395nm/), [Waveform Lighting (room blacklights)](https://www.waveformlighting.com/knowledgebase/uv-a-led-strip-lights/365nm-vs-395nm-which-is-recommended-for-room-black-lights)

### UV-A Safety for Continuous Viewing

| Consideration | Assessment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Eye safety** | Safe for indirect/ambient viewing | Avoid staring directly into LED for extended periods |
| **Skin safety** | Safe | UV-A at these intensities is far below sunlight UV-A exposure |
| **Long-term viewing** | 395nm may cause mild eye fatigue after prolonged staring | Not a significant concern for a desk accessory |
| **Photosensitive individuals** | Low risk at desktop lighting levels | Include note in manual for photosensitive medication users |
| **UV-A vs UV-C confusion** | Must be clearly communicated | Marketing must NEVER imply UV-A has germicidal properties |

**UV-A is safe for continuous operation during the entire grow cycle.** The dome can run UV-A 12 hours/day for the full grow duration without safety concerns. This is comparable to ambient UV-A exposure from indoor lighting and window light.

### Fluorescence Characteristics of Mushroom Species Under UV-A

| Species | Fluorescence Under UV-A | Visual Effect | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lion's Mane** | Mild autofluorescence from chitin in cell walls | Subtle blue-white glow on spine tips | Not strongly fluorescent; primary effect is UV illumination of white tissue against dark background |
| **Blue Oyster** | Mild to moderate | Blue-tinged glow; cap edges may fluoresce | Effect varies by growing conditions |
| **Pink Oyster** | Moderate | Coral-pink color is enhanced under UV-A; vibrant contrast | The pink pigmentation interacts well with UV-A for photography |
| **Shiitake** | Low | Dark caps absorb UV; gill edges may show faint fluorescence | Not a strong UV-A species visually |
| **Reishi** | Low to moderate | Lacquered surface may reflect UV-A; antler tips may glow faintly | Antler form reflects UV purple light dramatically |
| **Panellus stipticus** | N/A (bioluminescent) | Produces its own green glow without any UV | UV-A illumination competes with bioluminescence; Dark Dome (no LEDs) preferred |

**IMPORTANT NOTE:** The primary visual effect of UV-A on mushrooms is NOT strong fluorescence. Most gourmet mushroom species do not fluoresce intensely under UV-A the way fluorescent minerals or paints do. The visual appeal comes from:
1. The purple/violet ambient lighting creating a dramatic, otherworldly atmosphere
2. White and light-colored mushroom tissue (especially lion's mane spines) appearing to glow against the dark background
3. The contrast between the UV-lit mist/humidity and the organic mushroom forms
4. Chitin in fungal cell walls does exhibit autofluorescence (blue emission under UV excitation), but the effect is subtle and more pronounced under laboratory UV microscopy conditions than under consumer UV-A LEDs

**Uncertainty flag:** Specific fluorescence intensity data for each gourmet species under consumer-grade UV-A (365-395nm) LEDs is not well-documented in scientific literature. The visual effects described above are based on fungal autofluorescence research, photographer reports, and general knowledge of chitin fluorescence. Prototype testing with actual mushrooms under the dome's UV-A system is essential to validate the visual effect and tune LED placement/intensity.

### UV-A vs UV-C Comparison Summary

| Property | UV-A (Aesthetic) | UV-C (Germicidal) |
|---|---|---|
| **Wavelength** | 365-405nm | 200-280nm (dome uses 275nm) |
| **Purpose** | Visual: purple glow, atmosphere, photography | Functional: kill contaminants between grows |
| **Safety** | Safe for continuous viewing | **DANGEROUS** to eyes and skin |
| **Operation timing** | During active grow (12h on/off cycle) | ONLY between grows, dome sealed |
| **Effect on mushrooms** | None / beneficial (light trigger) | Harmful -- damages mycelium and fruiting bodies |
| **User interaction** | Automatic (timer-controlled) | Manual activation with safety interlocks |
| **Duration** | 12 hours/day throughout grow | 15-30 minutes per sterilization cycle |
| **Dome state** | Can be open or closed | **MUST be fully sealed** |
| **Visible to user** | Yes (purple glow is the product) | No (invisible; indicator LED only) |
| **Marketing name** | "UV lighting" / "ultraviolet glow" | "Sterilization cycle" / "chamber sterilization" |
